## Why this matters

Almost all patients with multiple myeloma eventually relapse, and lenalidomide-refractory disease at first relapse is now the rule rather than the exception because lenalidomide is used in both induction and maintenance for newly-diagnosed patients. The second-line setting has been reshaped over the past three years by three BCMA-directed therapeutic modalities reaching phase 3 readout: the antibody-drug conjugate belantamab mafodotin in the DREAMM-7 and DREAMM-8 trials, the CAR-T product ciltacabtagene autoleucel in CARTITUDE-4, and the bispecific T-cell engager teclistamab in MajesTEC-3.

## The three BCMA-directed modalities

| Modality | Example agent | T-cell dependent | Principal toxicity |
|---|---|---|---|
| ADC | Belantamab mafodotin | No | Ocular: keratopathy, blurred vision, dry eye |
| CAR-T | Ciltacabtagene autoleucel | Yes | CRS, ICANS, prolonged cytopenias, infection, parkinsonism |
| Bispecific | Teclistamab, elranatamab | Yes | CRS, ICANS, infection (CMV, opportunistic) |

The practical contrast at the bedside is straightforward. Belantamab mafodotin is a 30-minute intravenous infusion every 3 weeks with no mandated hospitalisation, no cytokine release syndrome, and no ICANS, but ocular adverse events occur in roughly 80% of treated patients with grade 3 or 4 events in approximately 40%. Most ocular events are reversible with dose modification. Cilta-cel is a single infusion preceded by apheresis, with a 4 to 6 week wait to infusion and bridging therapy for many patients. Teclistamab plus daratumumab is subcutaneous, off-the-shelf, with step-up dosing requiring inpatient monitoring and a notable infection signal.

## Verified efficacy benchmarks across the three phase 3 trials

| Trial | Regimen | Key finding | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| DREAMM-7 | BVd vs DVd | 36-month OS 74% vs 60%; HR 0.58 (95% CI 0.43 to 0.79); P = 0.00023 | Hungria 2025, Lancet Oncol |
| CARTITUDE-4 | Cilta-cel vs SoC | 30-month OS 76.4% vs 63.8%; HR 0.55 (95% CI 0.39 to 0.79); P = 0.0009 | Einsele 2026, Lancet Oncol |
| MajesTEC-3 | Tec + Dara vs DPd/DVd | 36-month PFS 83.4% vs 29.7%; HR 0.17 (95% CI 0.12 to 0.23); P < 0.001 | Costa 2026, NEJM |

DREAMM-7 and CARTITUDE-4 have both published overall survival superiority over standard of care in their respective populations. The MajesTEC-3 overall survival figure is not present in the published abstract and is therefore not retained as a verified benchmark in these notes; the 36-month progression-free survival figure stands. Cross-trial comparison is for orientation only. Patient populations and standard-of-care comparators differ: CARTITUDE-4 enrolled lenalidomide-refractory patients only, MajesTEC-3 enrolled patients largely naive to or non-refractory to anti-CD38 antibodies, and DREAMM-7 enrolled bortezomib-non-refractory patients.

## Belantamab mafodotin: where it stands in 2026

Belantamab mafodotin has had a complicated regulatory history. After the original 2020 FDA accelerated approval for monotherapy in heavily pretreated relapsed and refractory multiple myeloma was followed by withdrawal in 2022 to 2024 driven by the negative DREAMM-3 confirmatory trial, the agent has now regained approval in selected jurisdictions on the strength of DREAMM-7 and DREAMM-8.

In the UK, the MHRA approved belantamab mafodotin combinations on 17 April 2025: BVd after at least one prior therapy, and BPd after at least one prior therapy including lenalidomide. The European Commission approved the same combination indications in July 2025. The US FDA approved BVd on 23 October 2025 for adults with relapsed and refractory multiple myeloma after at least two prior lines of therapy including a proteasome inhibitor and an immunomodulatory agent, with a boxed warning and a REMS programme for ocular toxicity.

A practical point for clinical discussion: any teaching slide or letter that describes belantamab as "approved" should be qualified by year, jurisdiction, and line of therapy. Regulatory approval does not by itself guarantee NHS-funded access; local commissioning, NICE technology-appraisal status, and Trust formulary decisions should always be checked before clinical use.

## The "tale of two cities" and the emerging concept of functional cure

Functional cure, defined as durable remission off therapy with sustained MRD negativity and clinical stability, is now an open question rather than a fixed definition. The IMWG has not endorsed a single MRD threshold, and clinical trials use a range of sensitivities (10⁻⁵ to 10⁻⁶) and follow-up durations.

The strongest empirical signal currently available comes from the CARTITUDE-1 5-year follow-up published by Jagannath and colleagues in 2025. With a median follow-up of 61.3 months, 32 of 97 treated patients (33%) remained alive and progression-free at 5 years without maintenance treatment after a single cilta-cel infusion. Whether this represents functional cure remains a matter for longer follow-up and a formal consensus definition. The data should not be used to support a settled cure claim, but they are the clearest signal yet that a subset of heavily pretreated patients can achieve prolonged treatment-free remission.

A "tale of two cities" framing was used at the symposium to make the broader point. Two patients with similar baseline biology can have substantially different long-term trajectories depending on the depth of first-line therapy. The patient who reaches sustained MRD negativity after a quadruplet induction with anti-CD38 antibody, proteasome inhibitor, IMiD and dexamethasone, followed by transplant and maintenance, sits on a different long-term curve from the patient with the same risk profile who received a doublet. Access to optimal first-line therapy is itself one of the determinants of whether functional cure is achievable.

## Where this fits in clinical practice

The current BSH and UK Myeloma Society guideline reference for second-line and later management is the Jenner 2025 update in the British Journal of Haematology. The general sequencing principles align across that guideline and current NCCN guidance, with three practical anchors. Anti-CD38 retreatment is generally not recommended in patients with documented anti-CD38 refractory disease. Cross-modality activity within BCMA-directed therapies is described but the evidence remains early, with small case series suggesting bispecific antibodies retain activity after a prior BCMA-directed ADC. Selection between modalities at first relapse usually balances patient frailty and willingness to tolerate inpatient monitoring against outpatient deliverability and ocular tolerance.

The three clinical case applications in the full PDF work through how this plays out in practice: an anti-CD38 naive, lenalidomide-refractory patient with a strong preference for outpatient care; a 73-year-old with diabetes after six years on DRd; and a 67-year-old with triple-class exposure and aggressive high-risk relapse.
